§01 · The product
02 / 11

What is Toolskin, in one minute.

A single CSS file that turns any website, web app, or WordPress theme into a complete design system. Change one variable, the whole site re-themes. No build tools, no Node, no framework lock-in.

For developers

Token-driven CSS with derivative math. Change --ts-accent-h, the entire theme regenerates via color-mix() and clamp(). Drop one stylesheet, ship anywhere.

For designers

Figma variables that actually render in production. One token at the top, 240+ derived values cascade through every component automatically.

For agencies

50 unique client brands from one stylesheet. Stop redesigning from scratch. Token-swap your way to delivery in days, not weeks.

For non-technical buyers

A free design "engine" any website can use to look professional. Premium features, themes, and bundled solutions are paid.

10,000+ lines of CSS · OKLCH auto-contrast · WCAG AA built in · Zero dependencies
§02 · Market reality
03 / 11

The market changed in 2025. Plan accordingly.

Dying

Tailwind UI revenue collapsed 80%

75% of engineering team laid off in 2025. Cause: AI generators (v0, Cursor, Lovable, Bolt) made one-time-purchase component libraries obsolete.

"Tailwind is bigger than ever. Revenue is down 80%."
— Adam Wathan, founder · GitHub, 2025
Surviving

What still makes money in 2026

  • ✓Enterprise contracts with SLA + support
  • ✓Hosted services with recurring infrastructure
  • ✓Education, certification, books
  • ✓High-margin custom services
  • ✓Niches AI doesn't serve (WordPress GUI users)
Implication for Toolskin

Selling premium CSS components and templates as a primary revenue model is structurally weaker today than 18 months ago. The path to revenue is services first, productized later. Toolskin's value isn't selling the components – it's using them as a production engine to deliver client work faster than competitors.

Sources: Sacra · Contrary Research · Tailwind Labs founder statements · Yahoo Finance

§06 · Decision gates

Kill-switches. We commit to these now.

Honest pre-commitments are what makes this credible. We define exit criteria up front so we don't sunk-cost our way into a 12-month money pit.

DAY 14 · No signed contract
Pivot service positioning. Audit pricing/offer/audience. If still no traction by Day 21, kill service stream and go pure-product.
DAY 30 · Below $1.5K cumulative
We're below the conservative scenario floor. Reset entire approach. Possible pivot to consulting-only or shut down service ambitions.
DAY 60 · Below $5K cumulative
Project does not have product-service fit at current execution capacity. Either fund full-time hire or wind down to side-project status.
DAY 90 · Below $15K cumulative
Toolskin remains alive as open-source. Service business shutters. Founder pivots to employment or consulting elsewhere.

These are commitments to you, not predictions. The point is to remove emotional sunk-cost from the decision. We agree to them today – when the data is hypothetical and we have no skin in the game yet.

* Most failed founder partnerships fail because no one defined the exit criteria.

§09 · Defensibility

Why we win – and where we don't.

10 / 11
Defensible
  • ✓The token engine itself – derivative math, OKLCH, three-tier architecture. Hard to copy without rebuilding.
  • ✓Brand voice – industrial-precision tone. Differentiates instantly from soft alternatives.
  • ✓Founder credibility – established presence in WordPress automation tooling space.
  • ✓Speed advantage – Toolskin as production engine = 50% faster delivery than competitors.
  • ✓WordPress niche – AI generators don't serve GUI-driven WP users well.
Not defensible
  • ✗No network effects (CSS adoption isn't viral)
  • ✗Low switching costs (CSS is easy to swap)
  • ✗No capital advantage (we are not funded)
  • ✗No talent moat (anyone could rebuild this)
  • ✗AI eats the component market over time
The honest moat

Our advantage is execution speed × brand-niche specificity. We don't out-engineer Tailwind. We don't out-fund Vercel. We out-ship them in WordPress and adjacent SMB markets they don't bother targeting.

"We don't need a moat to start. We need a moat by year 2-3 to keep going."
